ULTRASOUND-GUIDED REGIONAL ANESTHESIA

Elbow Blocks · Wrist Blocks · Cutaneous Blocks · Bier Block

BRIEFING DOCUMENT

⭐ HIGH-YIELD CRNA BOARD SUMMARY

Organized by topic — mirrors the document sections below.

Blocks at the Elbow — General

  • Blocks at elbow vs. brachial plexus blocks: Elbow blocks carry GREATER risk of complications and are INFERIOR to brachial plexus block techniques — used when proximal block fails or for hand/wrist surgery only
  • Nerve injury risk: May INCREASE risk of anesthesia-related nerve injury compared to proximal brachial plexus blocks

Elbow Nerve Anatomy

  • Radial — cord origin: Posterior cord of brachial plexus
  • Radial — sensory: Dorsum of forearm and hand
  • Radial — motor: Dorsal forearm musculature
  • Median — cord origin: BOTH lateral AND medial cords of brachial plexus
  • Median — sensory: Radial palm, proximal fingers from thumb to long finger
  • Median — motor: Forearm flexors
  • Median — elbow position: Medial to brachial artery, anterior to brachialis muscle
  • Ulnar — cord origin: Medial cord of brachial plexus
  • Ulnar — sensory: Little finger
  • Ulnar — motor: Intrinsic muscles of hand
  • Ulnar — elbow location: Superficially in ulnar groove; posteromedial aspect of elbow

Ultrasound Appearances at the Elbow

  • Radial — US location: Deep between brachialis and brachioradialis; 3–4 cm above elbow crease on anterolateral arm
  • Radial — US appearance: Hyperechoic, triangular or oval shape with honeycomb appearance
  • Median — US appearance: Hyperechoic, oval — superficially located at elbow crease, medial to brachial artery
  • Ulnar — US appearance: Hyperechoic, honeycomb oval — superficially in ulnar groove, immediately under brachial fascia

Nerve Stimulation Endpoints and Volumes

  • Radial stim: 1.5 cm lateral to biceps tendon at epicondyle; forearm supinated/abducted; 3–5 mL local anesthetic; endpoint = hand paresthesia
  • Median stim: Needle medial to brachial artery at epicondyle; 3–5 mL; motor response = wrist flexion and/or thumb opposition
  • Ulnar stim: Needle 1 cm proximal to epicondyle directed distally; motor response = thumb adduction and/or finger flexion

⚠ Ulnar nerve — volume warning: Only 2–3 mL deposited — TIGHT fascial space = risk of compromised blood flow

Wrist Blocks

  • Nerves targeted: Radial, median, and ulnar nerves (including dorsal sensory branch of radial)
  • Radial at wrist: Field block (subcutaneous) 10 mL proximal to radial styloid — multiple branches present so field block used
  • Ulnar at wrist: 3–5 mL medial to and below flexor carpi ulnaris tendon; confirm NEGATIVE ASPIRATION due to proximity to ulnar artery
  • Median at wrist: Between flexor carpi radialis tendon and flexor palmaris longus tendon; inject 5 mL

⚠ Median nerve at wrist — key rule: Do NOT use epinephrine-containing solution — risk of ischemic injury

  • Wrist tourniquet: Wrist block does NOT require additional nerve blockade for wrist tourniquet placement
  • Toxicity risk: LOW — small volume at each location makes toxicity uncommon

Digital Block

  • 4 small nerves per digit; inject 2–3 mL at base of both sides of finger; do NOT use epinephrine

Cutaneous Blocks

  • Intercostobrachial nerve: T2 (sometimes T1) — NOT part of brachial plexus; sensation to upper medial/posterior arm and anterior axilla; used for tourniquet pain
  • Intercostobrachial technique: 5 mL subcutaneously superiorly AND inferiorly along axillary crease; subcutaneous = very few risks
  • Suprascapular nerve: C4–C5, branches from superior trunk of brachial plexus; innervates supraspinatus and infraspinatus; adjunct for shoulder procedures

Bier Block (Intravenous Regional Anesthesia)

  • Indication: Intravenous regional anesthesia — upper extremity surgery up to 1 hour; best for minimal postop pain procedures
  • Drug of choice: Dilute lidocaine (0.5%) 30–50 mL — do NOT use bupivacaine; do NOT use epinephrine-containing solution
  • Duration: Based on tourniquet duration — no need for long-acting local anesthetic
  • Tourniquet pressure: ~250 mmHg (approximately 100 mmHg above systolic blood pressure)
  • Minimum tourniquet time: Tourniquet must stay inflated MINIMUM 20 minutes after injection — allows local anesthetic absorption into tissues

⚠ LAST risk: Early deflation = local anesthetic enters systemic circulation → seizures and cardiovascular collapse

  • Deflation protocol: < 20 min: do not deflate | 20–40 min: deflate, reinflate, deflate at 1 min | > 40 min: deflate | 60 min: max
  • Tourniquet pain (25+ min): Inflate DISTAL cuff (over anesthetized area), then deflate PROXIMAL cuff
  • Ketorolac adjuvant: Ketorolac 15–30 mg can be added to solution for postoperative analgesia
  • Mechanism 1 (direct): Local anesthetic bathes bare nerve endings in tissue
  • Mechanism 2 (indirect/conduction): Local anesthetic transported to nerve substance via capillary network
  • Contraindications (absolute): Patient refusal only
  • Contraindications (relative): Crush injuries, no peripheral vein access, local skin infection, cellulitis, compound fractures, local anesthetic allergy, severe vascular injury, severe peripheral vascular disease, sickle cell disease, surgery > 1 hour
  • Lower extremity IVRA: Requires LARGER local anesthetic volume (increased LAST risk) and HIGHER tourniquet pressure

1. OVERVIEW — BLOCKS AT THE ELBOW

  • Greater risk of complications than brachial plexus block techniques
  • Inferior to proximal brachial plexus blocks — more challenging to perform
  • May increase risk of anesthesia-related nerve injury
  • Indications:
  • Hand and wrist surgery
  • Supplement to a failed proximal brachial plexus block
  • Pain treatment of minor trauma or surgery

3. RADIAL NERVE AT THE ELBOW

Anatomy

  • Origin: posterior cord of brachial plexus
  • Sensory: dorsum of forearm and hand
  • Motor: musculature of dorsal forearm
  • Location at elbow: lies deep between brachialis and brachioradialis muscles at the epicondyle

Ultrasound-Guided Technique

  • Transducer: anterolateral aspect of distal arm, 3–4 cm above elbow crease
  • Needle: in-plane; traverse brachioradialis muscle, place tip next to radial nerve
  • Appearance: hyperechoic, triangular or oval shape with honeycomb appearance
  • Can also block branches of radial nerve in the forearm

Nerve Stimulation Technique

  • Patient supine, forearm supinated and abducted
  • Needle: 1.5 cm lateral to biceps tendon at epicondyle
  • Endpoint: hand paresthesia
  • Volume: 3–5 mL local anesthetic

4. MEDIAN NERVE AT THE ELBOW

Anatomy

  • Origin: lateral AND medial cords of brachial plexus
  • Sensory: radial palm, proximal fingers from thumb to long finger
  • Motor: forearm flexors
  • Location: medial to brachial artery, anterior to brachialis muscle

Ultrasound-Guided Technique

  • Transducer: transverse, just superior to antecubital fossa — must visualize brachial artery as landmark
  • Location: superficially at elbow crease, medial to brachial artery
  • Appearance: hyperechoic, oval-shaped structure
  • Needle: in-plane from medial to lateral

Nerve Stimulation Technique

  • Needle: medial to brachial artery at epicondyle
  • Motor response: wrist flexion and/or thumb opposition OR paresthesia
  • Volume: 3–5 mL local anesthetic

5. ULNAR NERVE AT THE ELBOW

Anatomy

  • Origin: medial cord of brachial plexus
  • Sensory: little finger
  • Motor: intrinsic muscles of hand
  • Location: superficially in ulnar groove

Ultrasound-Guided Technique

  • Transducer: posteromedial aspect of elbow, a few cm proximal to elbow crease; move medially in antecubital fossa until nerve visualized
  • Appearance: hyperechoic, honeycomb oval immediately underneath the brachial fascia
  • Needle: in-plane from either side of transducer

Nerve Stimulation Technique

  • Patient supine, forearm flexed at elbow
  • Needle: inserted 1 cm proximal to epicondyle, directed distally
  • Endpoint: paresthesia to little finger OR motor response (thumb adduction, finger flexion)

⚠ Only 2–3 mL local anesthetic — tight fascial space; larger volumes risk compromised blood flow

6. BLOCKS AT THE WRIST

Overview

  • Block radial, median, and ulnar nerves — including dorsal sensory branch of radial nerve
  • Simple to perform; effective for hand and finger procedures
  • Toxicity uncommon — small volumes injected at each location
  • Wrist tourniquet does NOT require additional nerve blockade

Radial Nerve at the Wrist

  • Landmark: radial styloid
  • Technique: subcutaneous field block of 10 mL proximal to radial styloid
  • Field block used because multiple branches present — no single nerve to target

Ulnar Nerve at the Wrist

  • Landmarks: ulnar styloid, ulnar pulse, flexor carpi ulnaris tendon
  • Technique: inject 3–5 mL medial to and below flexor carpi ulnaris tendon

⚠ Confirm negative aspiration — ulnar artery is in close proximity

Median Nerve at the Wrist

  • Landmarks: flexor carpi radialis tendon and flexor palmaris longus tendon
  • Technique: inject 5 mL between the two tendons

⚠ Do NOT use epinephrine-containing solution — risk of ischemic injury

  • Wrist tourniquet does NOT require additional nerve blockade

7. DIGITAL BLOCK

  • Each digit innervated by 4 small nerves
  • Technique: inject 2–3 mL at base of both sides of finger — note proximity of digital arteries

⚠ Do NOT use epinephrine — risk of digital ischemia

8. CUTANEOUS BLOCKS OF THE UPPER EXTREMITY

Supraclavicular Nerve Block

  • Origin: C3, C4
  • Often anesthetized during interscalene block — NOT reliably during supraclavicular block
  • Technique: 3 mL in mid-belly of sternocleidomastoid, then 2–7 mL along posterior border of sternocleidomastoid

Suprascapular Nerve Block

  • Origin: C4, C5 — superior trunk of brachial plexus
  • Typically anesthetized during interscalene block
  • Motor: innervates supraspinatus and infraspinatus muscles
  • Indication: adjunct for shoulder procedures

Intercostobrachial Nerve Block

  • Origin: T2 (sometimes T1) — NOT part of the brachial plexus
  • Sensory: upper half of medial/posterior arm and anterior axilla
  • Indications: surgery in area, tourniquet sensory pain, anterior arthroscopic port placement
  • Addresses sensory component of tourniquet pain only — does NOT address ischemic/compressive component
  • Technique: 5 mL subcutaneously superiorly AND inferiorly along axillary crease
  • Safety: subcutaneous placement = very few risks

Lateral and Medial Antebrachial Cutaneous Nerve Block

  • Lateral antebrachial cutaneous nerve: arises from musculocutaneous nerve
  • Medial antebrachial cutaneous nerve: arises from medial cord of brachial plexus
  • Indication: forearm surgery or incomplete brachial plexus block
  • Always blocked together

9. BIER BLOCK (INTRAVENOUS REGIONAL ANESTHESIA)

Overview and Indications

  • Intravenous regional anesthesia for upper extremity surgery up to 1 hour
  • Examples: carpal tunnel release, ganglionectomy, Dupuytren's contracture surgery, fracture reductions
  • Best for procedures that produce minimal postoperative pain

Mechanism of Action

  • Mechanism 1 — Direct (peripheral infiltration): local anesthetic bathes bare nerve endings directly in tissue fluid
  • Mechanism 2 — Indirect (conduction block): local anesthetic transported into the substance of the nerves via the capillary network (vasa nervorum) — produces a true conduction block despite intravenous injection

Drug Selection

  • Drug: dilute 0.5% lidocaine — 30–50 mL
  • Duration based on tourniquet duration — no need for long-acting local anesthetic

⚠ Do NOT use bupivacaine

⚠ Do NOT use epinephrine-containing solution

  • Ketorolac 15–30 mg can be added to solution for postoperative analgesia
  • IV catheter management: can be removed or kept in place if it is out of the surgical field

Tourniquet Management

  • Type: double pneumatic tourniquet on upper arm
  • Inflation pressure: approximately 250 mmHg (about 100 mmHg above systolic blood pressure)
  • Minimum inflation time: 20 minutes after injection — allows local anesthetic to absorb into tissues

⚠ Deflating before 20 minutes = local anesthetic enters systemic circulation → seizures and cardiovascular collapse

Step-by-Step Procedure

  • 1. Intravenous catheter inserted as far distally as possible (22 gauge sufficient)
  • 2. Double pneumatic tourniquet placed on upper arm
  • 3. Arm elevated 1–2 minutes for passive exsanguination
  • 4. Esmarch bandage applied distal to proximal for active exsanguination
  • 5. Inflate DISTAL cuff (further exsanguinates)
  • 6. Inflate PROXIMAL cuff
  • 7. Deflate DISTAL cuff
  • 8. Remove Esmarch bandage
  • 9. Inject 30–50 mL of 0.5% lidocaine
  • 10. Tourniquet pain at 25+ minutes: inflate distal cuff over now-anesthetized area, then deflate proximal cuff

Tourniquet Deflation Protocol

TIME SINCE INJECTION ACTION
< 20 minutes Do NOT deflate — wait until 20 minutes have elapsed
20–40 minutes Deflate, immediately reinflate to assess for signs and symptoms of LAST, then deflate again at 1 minute
> 40 minutes Deflate
60 minutes Generally agreed upon maximum — do not exceed

Contraindications

Absolute

  • Patient refusal

Relative

  • Crush injuries
  • Inability to locate peripheral veins
  • Local skin infection or cellulitis
  • Compound fractures
  • Convincing history of local anesthetic allergy
  • Severe vascular injuries or severe peripheral vascular disease
  • Sickle cell disease
  • Surgery expected to exceed 1 hour

Lower Extremity IVRA

  • Larger local anesthetic volume required → increased risk of local anesthetic systemic toxicity
  • Higher tourniquet pressure required

Elbow blocks are inferior to brachial plexus techniques and carry higher nerve injury risk — always prefer proximal blocks when possible.
