⭐ HIGH-YIELD CRNA BOARD SUMMARY

Most testable facts for CRNA boards — review before exam.

TOPIC HIGH-YIELD FACT
PNB advantages ↓ opioid requirements, ↓ surgical stress response, ↓ need for GA/ETT, ↓ postop pain
Ideal PNB candidates OSA, PONV history, chronic pain, respiratory depression risk from GA, painful surgeries
Emergency meds for PNB Atropine, glycopyrrolate, ephedrine, phenylephrine, succinylcholine, propofol, 20% Intralipid — all must be immediately available
Premedication regimen Midazolam 0.5–3 mg + Fentanyl 25–100 mcg ± Propofol 20 mg
Needle gauge (single shot) 22-gauge most common for single-shot PNBs; 17–19g for catheter placement
Needle bevel Short bevel (45°) = LESS likely to enter fascicle but causes MORE damage if it does; Long bevel = opposite
Nerve stim: safe threshold <0.5 mA = close needle-nerve relationship; <0.2 mA = likely intraneural → withdraw immediately
Nerve stim parameters Pulse width: 0.1 msec; Frequency: 0.5–4 Hz (2 Hz most common); Amplitude: assesses distance
Injection pressure limit <15 psi = safe; >20 psi = intrafascicular injection; HIGH pressure also risks epidural spread
Ultrasound advantage Gold standard; direct nerve visualization; smaller LA volume needed; works in paralyzed/amputee patients
Intraneural injection volumes As little as 0.1–0.5 mL injected INTO fascicle can cause fascicle & perineurium rupture
Double Crush Phenomenon Two low-grade insults along a nerve trunk produce damage far exceeding additive effect of each alone (Upton & McComas)
PNI mechanisms Mechanical/traumatic, vascular/ischemic, chemical/neurotoxic, inflammatory — mechanical fascicular injury is most common
LAST onset Majority of LAST cases occur within 1 min of injection
LAST: CNS symptoms (early) Perioral numbness → tinnitus → agitation → dysarthria → confusion → SEIZURES → coma
LAST: CV progression Tachycardia/HTN → bradycardia/hypotension → ventricular arrhythmias/asystole
LAST seizure treatment IV benzodiazepines are MAINSTAY; propofol (caution: hypotension) or small paralytic PRN
LAST: drugs to AVOID Vasopressin, lidocaine for arrhythmias, beta-blockers, CCBs — all worsen cardiac toxicity
Epinephrine dose in LAST Keep <1 mcg/kg (higher doses may hinder lipid emulsion effectiveness)
20% Lipid Emulsion (ILE) Acts as 'lipid sink' to scavenge LA + cardiotonic effect → ↑ CO; must be available wherever LA blocks performed
Injection site toxicity order IV > tracheal > intercostal > caudal > epidural > brachial plexus > sciatic/femoral (highest → lowest absorption)
Intravascular injection prevention Incremental injection + frequent aspiration; caution with transducer pressure
Documentation required Premedication, sterile technique, monitoring methods, equipment, LA type/volume/adjuvants, clinician, patient response, nerve appearance
Consent Written consent required; brief, include benefits AND risks; hard copy improves patient recall
Proximal vs distal PNI Proximal injections = higher nerve injury risk; distal axonal lesions = higher re-innervation likelihood
Diabetic neuropathy + PNB Higher PNI incidence; may be more susceptible to LA neurotoxicity d/t chronic ischemic hypoxia

8. INTRANEURAL INJECTION — PREVENTION SUMMARY

METHOD DETAILS
Paresthesia elicitation NOT a good prevention method. If paresthesia encountered → prompt cessation + repositioning. Caution with sedated patients!
Nerve Stimulation <0.5 mA = close relationship; <0.2 mA = HIGH specificity for intraneural. Historically pre-US; now used WITH US to confirm.
Opening Pressure Avoid >15 psi. High injection pressure also → undesired spread (e.g., epidural). Use compressed air technique or in-line manometer.
Electrical Impedance ↑ when moving from extraneural to intraneural. May detect intravascular. Useful but widespread use has NOT decreased PNI rate.
Ultrasound Current best practice. Real-time needle visualization. Higher resolution in future may allow intra- vs extra-fascicular differentiation.
