BRACHIAL PLEXUS ANATOMY

BRIEFING DOCUMENT

HIGH-YIELD SUMMARY: CRNA BOARD-TESTABLE FACTS

STRUCTURE & ORGANIZATION

  • Brachial plexus: ventral rami of C5-C8 & T1 (small contributions from C4 & T2)
  • Components: 5 roots → 3 trunks → 6 divisions → 3 cords → 5 terminal branches
  • Mnemonic: "Randy Travis Drinks Cold Beer" (Roots, Trunks, Divisions, Cords, Branches) — "5 3 6 3 5"
  • Supraclavicular = roots + trunks + divisions (posterior triangle of neck)
  • Infraclavicular = cords + terminal branches (axilla)

KEY NERVE ROOTS & CLINICAL CORRELATES

  • Phrenic nerve (C3, C4, C5): almost always blocked during interscalene block → hemidiaphragmatic paralysis
  • Long thoracic nerve (C5-C7): serratus anterior; injury → winged scapula
  • Dorsal scapular nerve (C5): levator scapulae & rhomboids

TERMINAL BRANCHES

  • Musculocutaneous (C5-C7), Median (C6-T1), Ulnar (C8-T1), Radial (C5-T1), Axillary (C5-C6)
  • Cords named by relationship to axillary artery
  • Posterior cord mnemonic "STARS": Subscapular (upper), Thoracodorsal, Axillary, Radial, Subscapular (lower)

INJURY PATTERNS (HIGH-YIELD)

  • Erb-Duchenne palsy (C5-C6): "waiter's tip" — adducted shoulder, medially rotated arm, extended elbow, pronated forearm, flexed wrist
  • Radial nerve injury: wrist drop (IM deltoid injection or tight cast over mid-humerus)
  • Median nerve injury: numbness/tingling palm & lateral 3.5 digits, weak pronation & thumb movements
  • Ulnar nerve injury: "claw hand" — inability to abduct/adduct digits, interosseous atrophy, loss of sensation medial 1.5 digits
  • Long thoracic nerve injury: winged scapula, cannot abduct arm beyond horizontal

BLOCK RELEVANCE

  • Interscalene block: targets trunks (supraclavicular); almost always blocks phrenic nerve
  • Supraclavicular block: targets divisions at level of 1st rib; less frequent phrenic block
  • Axillary block: targets terminal branches
  • Intercostobrachial nerve (T2): NOT part of BP — may require separate field block for tourniquet/medial arm surgery
  • BP blocks won't cover distal clavicular fractures (supraclavicular nerve C3-C4, not part of BP)

BRACHIAL PLEXUS OVERVIEW

  • Origin: ventral rami of C5-C8 & T1 spinal nerves
  • Small contributions may be made by C4 & T2 nerves
  • Supplies: entire upper limb except trapezius (spinal accessory nerve) and skin of medial upper arm (intercostobrachial nerve)
  • Ventral rami: sensory and motor to periphery, limbs, and trunk of body
  • Components: 5 roots → 3 trunks → 6 divisions (2 per trunk, ant/post) → 3 cords → 5 terminal branches
  • Mnemonic: "Randy Travis Drinks Cold Beer" — "5 3 6 3 5"
  • (C8? — Note: C8 spinal nerve exits below C7 vertebra; there is no C8 vertebra)

1. ROOTS

  • Roots line the interval between the anterior and middle scalene muscles
  • 3 non-terminal branches arise at the root level:
  • Dorsal scapular nerve (C5) — innervates levator scapulae & rhomboids
  • Phrenic nerve (C3, C4, C5) — "keeps the diaphragm alive"
  • C3-C4 contribution not technically part of BP but receives C5 contribution & is anatomically relevant
  • Long thoracic nerve (C5, C6, C7) — innervates serratus anterior muscle
  • Each root has an associated dermatome and myotome

Phrenic Nerve — Clinical Significance

  • Almost always blocked during interscalene block & less frequently with supraclavicular block
  • Resulting in hemidiaphragmatic paralysis → respiratory compromise in patients with poor pulmonary reserve

CAUTION: Interscalene block contraindicated in patients dependent on unilateral diaphragmatic function

2. TRUNKS

  • Formed after nerves pass the lateral border of the scalene muscles
  • Pass through the posterior triangle of the neck
  • Level of interscalene block

Trunk Composition

  • C5-C6 → Upper (superior) trunk
  • Only trunk that gives off two non-terminal branches:
  • Suprascapular nerve (C5) — innervates supraspinatus m., infraspinatus m., posterior glenohumeral joint, subacromial bursa, & AC joint
  • Subclavius nerve (C5-C6) — subclavius muscle
  • C7 → Middle trunk
  • C8-T1 → Lower (inferior) trunk

3. DIVISIONS

  • Trunks divide into divisions at the lateral border of the 1st rib, posterior to the clavicle
  • Each trunk divides into 2 divisions: anterior & posterior (6 total)
  • Level of supraclavicular block
  • Pass behind the clavicle
  • No non-terminal branches at the division level

Division Composition

  • C5-C6: Superior trunk → anterior & posterior divisions
  • C7: Middle trunk → anterior & posterior divisions
  • C8-T1: Inferior trunk → anterior & posterior divisions

Anatomical Division by Clavicle

  • Supraclavicular: 5 nerve roots, 3 trunks, 6 divisions — posterior triangle of neck
  • Infraclavicular: 3 cords & terminal branches — within the axilla

4. CORDS

  • In the axilla, divisions unite to form cords
  • Named in relationship to the axillary artery (lateral is lateral to axillary artery, etc.)

Lateral Cord (C5-C7)

  • Formed by anterior divisions of superior & middle trunks

Posterior Cord (C5-T1)

  • All 3 posterior divisions unite

Medial Cord (C8-T1)

  • Formed by anterior division of inferior trunk

5. BRANCHES OF THE CORDS

Note: Lateral & medial cords predominantly supply ventral portions of the upper extremity; posterior cord branches predominantly supply the dorsal portion.

Lateral Cord — 3 Branches

  • Lateral pectoral nerve — pectoralis major m.
  • Musculocutaneous nerve (terminal)
  • Median nerve (lateral root) (terminal)

Posterior Cord — 5 Branches (Mnemonic: "STARS")

  • Subscapular (upper) — subscapularis m.
  • Thoracodorsal — latissimus dorsi m.
  • Axillary (terminal)
  • Radial (terminal)
  • Subscapular (lower) — subscapularis m.

Medial Cord — 5 Branches

  • Ulnar nerve (terminal)
  • Medial pectoral nerve (C8-T1) — pectoralis major & minor mm.
  • Medial cutaneous nerve of forearm (medial antebrachial cutaneous n.) — sensory
  • Medial cutaneous nerve of arm (medial brachial cutaneous n.) — sensory
  • Median nerve (medial root) (terminal)

6. TERMINAL NERVES / BRANCHES

  • Targeted at the level of an axillary block
Terminal Nerve Root Value Cord Origin
Musculocutaneous C5-C7 Lateral
Median C6-T1 Lateral + Medial
Ulnar C8-T1 Medial
Radial C5-T1 Posterior
Axillary C5-C6 Posterior

7. INNERVATION REVIEW

You must know this: Sensory, Motor, Osteotomes, Clinical Assessment, Injury Assessment

Sensory Innervation (Dermatomes)

  • Dermatomes correspond to dorsal sensory nerve roots
  • Each root has an associated dermatomal distribution over the upper extremity

Motor Innervation (Myotomes)

  • Myotomes correspond to ventral spinal nerve roots
  • Contrast: dermatomes = sensory; osteotomes = bone innervation; myotomes = motor
  • Do not necessarily correlate with dermatomes (skin) or osteotomes (bone)

Osteotomes

  • Bone innervation — correspond to dorsal spinal nerve roots
  • Supraclavicular nerve (C3-C4): BP blocks won't cover distal clavicular fractures

8. INTERCOSTOBRACHIAL NERVE (T2)

NOT a part of the Brachial Plexus

  • Arises from the 2nd intercostal nerve
  • Sensory innervation of medial aspect of upper arm [Note: "innveration" in original]
  • May require a field block for surgery or tourniquet

9. INJURY ASSESSMENT

Erb-Duchenne Palsy — Superior Roots (C5-C6)

  • "Waiter's tip" position
  • Caused by forceful pulling of head from shoulder (heavy fall on shoulder, excessive stretching of infant's neck during childbirth)
  • Presentation:
  • Adducted shoulder
  • Medial rotation of arm
  • Extended elbow
  • Pronated forearm
  • Flexed wrist
  • Lateral arm numbness

Radial Nerve Injury

  • May result from IM deltoid injection or tight cast over mid-humerus
  • Results in wrist drop
  • Sensory loss minimal d/t overlap of adjacent nerves

Median Nerve Injury

  • Numbness, tingling, & pain in palm & lateral 3.5 digits
  • Inability to pronate forearm & flex proximal interphalangeal joints of all digits & distal interphalangeal joints of 2nd & 3rd digits
  • Wrist flexion weak, accompanied by adduction
  • Thumb movements weak

Ulnar Nerve Injury

  • Inability to abduct or adduct digits
  • Atrophy of interosseous muscles of hand
  • Hyperextension of metacarpophalangeal joints
  • Flexion of interphalangeal joints — "claw hand"
  • Loss of sensation over medial 1.5 digits

Long Thoracic Nerve Injury

  • Paralysis of serratus anterior muscle
  • Results in "winged scapula"
  • Arm cannot be abducted beyond horizontal position
