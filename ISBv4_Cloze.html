INTERSCALENE BRACHIAL PLEXUS BLOCKBRIEFING DOCUMENT

★ HIGH YIELD

Critical Safety Thresholds

NEVER inject if motor response obtained at <0.2 mA → intraneural injection → retrograde flow → TOTAL SPINAL ANESTHESIA

  • Never advance needle >2.5 cm → cervical cord injury, pneumothorax, vascular puncture
  • Never inject with excessive pressure (>15 psi) → stop if pt reports severe pain or withdrawal reaction
  • PNS setting >1.0 mA not necessary → too high, causes exaggerated response and pt discomfort
  • Goal stimulation: ~0.5 mA (0.1 ms)

When NOT to Use ISB

ABSOLUTE CONTRAINDICATIONS: patient refusal, local infection, active bleeding in anticoagulated pt, allergy to LA

  • NOT for surgery below the elbow (ulnar sparing; C8–T1 frequently spared) → not for forearm, hand, or elbow unless inferior trunk blockade confirmed on US
  • Relative CI: COPD (phrenic blockade ↓ pulmonary function ~25% — nearly 100% of ISBs cause hemidiaphragmatic paralysis)
  • Relative CI: contralateral phrenic or RLN paresis → bilateral phrenic block → respiratory failure
  • Pre-existing neuro deficits in arm to be blocked — must assess and document before procedure

NEVER direct needle cephalad → risk of cervical spinal cord puncture

Must-Know Numbers

  • ~100% ipsilateral hemidiaphragmatic paralysis
  • ~25% ↓ in pulmonary function from phrenic blockade
  • PNS: 0.8–1.0 mA start → goal ~0.5 mA → stop if <0.2 mA
  • LA volume PNS: 25–35 mL | US-guided: 15–20 mL (now often 10–15 mL)
  • Max needle depth: 2.5 cm | Needle insertion: 3–4 cm above clavicle | Plexus depth on US: 1–3 cm
  • Drug of choice: Ropivacaine 0.5% or 0.75%
  • Catheter duration: up to 60 hrs
  • Brachial plexus roots: C5–T1 (memorize; Hadzic says C6–T1 but all major texts = C5–T1)

Key Clinical Pearls

  • Preferred motor response: deltoid twitch (axillary N → shoulder abduction); diaphragm = too anterior; trapezius = too posterior
  • US “stoplight” view = snowman = vertical alignment of 3 trunks at ~C6; target for US-guided ISB
  • Horner’s syndrome triad: miosis, anhidrosis, ptosis (“oculosympathetic palsy”) — expected side effect, not an emergency
  • Bezold-Jarisch Reflex (HBE): sudden bradycardia + hypotension (also apnea, cardiac arrest in severe cases); favored by sitting position + hypovolemia; Tx = atropine + ephedrine; can occur in regional, UE blocks, and GA
  • Hoarseness: RLN block; more common on right (RLN loops under subclavian R → more superficial)
  • LAST: vertebral artery at C6 — injection → rapid seizures; have Intralipid, airway equipment, crash cart ready
  • Sniff test: forceful sniff tenses scalenes → fall into interscalene groove; landmark trick for difficult anatomy

OVERVIEW

  • Targets the trunks (some sources: roots) of the brachial plexus at the C6 level within the interscalene groove
  • Relies on spread of large LA volume to block BP; results in anesthesia of supraclavicular nerves
  • Superficial, easy to perform, comfortable for pt; single-shot or catheter (up to 60 hrs)
  • NOT recommended for surgery below the elbow (ulnar sparing; C8–T1 sparing common)
  • Covers: Anatomy → Indications → Contraindications → Positioning → PNS Technique → US-Guided Technique → Side Effects & Complications

1. ANATOMY

Brachial Plexus

  • Roots C5–T1 (memorize C5–T1; Hadzic says C6–T1 — all other major texts agree C5–T1)
  • Brachial plexus anatomy is essential knowledge for this course, the SEE/NCE board exams, and clinical practice; brachial plexus level correlates with specific nerve block indications
  • Interscalene groove = potential space between anterior and middle scalene muscles
  • Plexus lateral to carotid artery; visualized at depth of 1–3 cm on US

Key Nearby Structures

  • Phrenic nerve (C3–C5): runs on anterior surface of anterior scalene → blocked nearly 100% of time
  • Stellate ganglion / sympathetic chain → Horner's syndrome if blocked
  • Recurrent laryngeal nerve (RLN) → hoarseness if blocked; more common on right
  • Vertebral artery: passes posteriorly at C6 (visible on US) → seizures if injected
  • Supraclavicular nerves (cervical plexus, C3–4): innervate skin over and medial to the acromion, cephalad shoulder/clavicle; blocked via LA spill into prevertebral fascia

Sensory Distribution — Clinical Pearls

  • Arthroscopic shoulder surgery: key nerves = supraclavicular, suprascapular, axillary (radial)
  • Open shoulder surgery: additionally consider medial cutaneous n. of arm, intercostobrachial n., medial cutaneous n. of forearm
  • Medial cutaneous n. of arm = medial antebrachial cutaneous n. — both C8, T1; group with ulnar → may be spared with ISB
  • Suprascapular n. (C5) = non-terminal branch of upper trunk; innervates supraspinatus, infraspinatus, posterior GH joint, subacromial bursa, AC joint

2. INDICATIONS

  • Surgery of the shoulder, proximal humerus, distal 2/3 of clavicle
  • Shoulder arthroscopy, rotator cuff repair, open shoulder surgery, Bankart repair, acromioplasty
  • Single-shot or catheter placement
  • NOT recommended for elbow, forearm, or hand surgery
    • → Potential sparing of inferior trunk; frequent sparing of C8–T1 (ulnar border of forearm)
    • → Unless inferior trunk blockade confirmed via US-guided low-interscalene approach
  • Relies on spread of large LA volume within the interscalene groove; ultrasound guidance is changing volume requirements (lower volumes now achievable)
  • Note: some texts also state ISB should not be used for forearm or elbow surgery (not just hand) unless inferior trunk blockade is certain via US-guided low-interscalene approach

3. ADVANTAGES / DISADVANTAGES

Advantages

  • → Anesthesia of supraclavicular nerves
  • Superficial, easy to perform, comfortable for pt
  • Targets trunks of brachial plexus [Apex says roots — it's a continuous structure]

Disadvantages

  • Hemidiaphragmatic paralysis — nearly 100%; phrenic nerve paralysis occurs nearly 100% of the time; caution in pts with respiratory compromise; can cause SOB
  • Not recommended for elbow, forearm, or hand surgery (ulnar sparing)
  • Horner's syndrome

4. CONTRAINDICATIONS

Absolute

  • Patient refusal
  • Local infection at site
  • Active bleeding in anticoagulated patient
  • Allergy to LA

Relative

  • COPD (phrenic blockade ↓ pulmonary function ~25%)
  • Contralateral paresis of phrenic nerve or RLN
  • Pre-existing neuro deficits in arm to be blocked — assess pre-procedure
  • Patient cooperation limitations

5. PATIENT POSITIONING & LANDMARK IDENTIFICATION

Positioning

  • Supine or semi-sitting; head turned away from block side; arm resting at side

Primary Landmarks

  • Clavicle
  • Posterior border of clavicular head of SCM
  • External jugular vein (EJV) — usually crosses interscalene groove at level of trunks
  • Groove often palpated just anterior or posterior to EJV

Facilitating Landmark Identification

  • Ask pt to raise head off table → accentuates SCM
  • Valsalva maneuver → accentuates EJV
  • Ask pt to sniff forcefully while palpating groove → tenses scalene muscles; helps identify groove
  • Ask pt to reach toward ipsilateral knee → flattens neck skin

6. PNS TECHNIQUE

Performed at level of C6 vertebral body. Covers upper roots innervating shoulder muscles.

Steps

  • Clean skin with antiseptic
  • Sub-q infiltrate needle site with 1–3 mL LA
    • ⚠ BP is very shallow here — deeper sub-q insertion can deposit LA into plexus → nerve damage / difficulty obtaining motor response
  • Set PNS: 0.8–1.0 mA, 2 Hz, 0.1 ms
  • Insert needle 3–4 cm above clavicle, perpendicular to skin plane in caudal direction — NEVER cephalad (risk: cervical cord puncture)
  • Advance slowly until BP stimulation occurs (usually 1–2 cm)
  • Inject 25–35 mL of LA solution

Goal Stimulation

  • Target: ~0.5 mA (0.1 ms)

Acceptable Motor Responses

  • Musculocutaneous N → biceps → elbow flexion
  • Radial N → triceps → elbow extension
  • Pectoral N → pectoralis → shoulder adduction
  • Axillary N → deltoid → shoulder abduction (**preferred**)
  • Terminal branches → hand/forearm movement

Unacceptable Responses

  • Diaphragm (phrenic N) → redirect posteriorly
  • Trapezius (spinal accessory N) → redirect anteriorly
  • Dorsal scapular / levator scapulae → redirect anteriorly

7. ULTRASOUND-GUIDED ISB

Positioning & Landmarks

  • Supine, semisitting, or semilateral decubitus (rolled blanket/towel); head away from block side; slight head elevation (↑ venous drainage)
  • US landmarks: cricoid cartilage (C6), SCM, superior clavicle

US Anatomy

  • "Stoplight" (snowman) view = vertical alignment of 3 trunks at ~C6 level
  • Plexus lateral to carotid artery; between anterior and middle scalene muscles; depth 1–3 cm
  • Inferior trunk: anesthetize via deeper injection or redirect needle inferiorly

Technique — 2 Options

  • Scan and identify anatomy before needle insertion
  • Ensure sterility of probe cover; disinfect skin
  • Localize with 1–2% lidocaine skin wheal at needle insertion site
  • US advantage: allows multiple injections around the plexus; may allow for reduced volumes (15–25 mL adequate; now often 10–15 mL achievable)
  • Option 1: Transducer at level of cricoid (C6) → identify carotid → move laterally → identify scalene muscles → BP wedged between anterior and middle scalene
  • Option 2: Transducer on superior mid-clavicle → identify subclavian artery + BP → move probe cephalad until stoplight view (~C6)

Injection

  • Insert needle lateral → medial
  • If PNS used: motor response of shoulder/arm/forearm confirms groove entry
  • Keep needle tip in view at all times
  • Volume: 15–20 mL Ropivacaine 0.5% or 0.75% (now can use 10–15 mL with US)
  • Aspirate before injection and every 5–10 mL
  • Watch for good LA spread around plexus
  • Emergency equipment immediately available: airway, crash cart, Intralipid, benzos

8. TIPS & SAFETY

  • Never advance needle >2.5 cm → risk: cervical cord injury, pneumo, vascular puncture
  • Never inject with resistance/high pressure → withdraw or rotate needle slightly
  • PNS >1.0 mA not necessary → too high; exaggerated response, pt discomfort

NEVER inject if motor response elicited at <0.2 mA → intraneural injection risk → peripheral nerve injury (PNI); retrograde flow → neuraxial space → TOTAL SPINAL ANESTHESIA

  • Monitor twitching carefully — may need to place hand on muscle to confirm
  • Avoid fast, forceful LA injection
  • ↓ volumes in elderly/frail pts
  • Never inject with excessive pressure (>15 psi)
  • Stop immediately if pt reports severe pain or withdrawal reaction on injection

9. SIDE EFFECTS & COMPLICATIONS

Hemidiaphragmatic Paralysis

  • Caused by phrenic nerve blockade (C3–C5)
  • Ipsilateral blockade occurs ~100% of the time (rarely symptomatic clinically)
  • ↓ pulmonary function ~25% — caution in significant pulmonary disease pts

Horner's Syndrome ("Oculosympathetic Palsy")

  • Blockade of sympathetic cervical ganglion chain including stellate ganglion
  • Classic triad: miosis, anhidrosis, ptosis
  • Also: hyperemia of conjunctiva, enophthalmos, nasal congestion, vasodilation, ↑ facial temp

Hoarseness

  • RLN blockade; more common on R than L (RLN loops under subclavian on right → more superficial)

Pneumothorax

  • Needle directed too far caudally (R > L)

Suspect if pt c/o chest pain or cough during needle placement — have plan ready

  • Clinical question: How would you proceed if you suspect a pneumothorax? (Have an answer — monitor SpO2, CXR, consider needle decompression if tension; do not leave pt unattended)

Bezold-Jarisch Reflex (Hypotensive-Bradycardic Episodes, HBEs)

  • Cardioinhibitory reflex: sudden bradycardia & hypotension
  • Favored by: sitting position, awake pt, hypovolemia
  • Tx: atropine + ephedrine
  • Prevention: avoid hypovolemia
  • Mechanism: cardioinhibitory receptors in the walls of the ventricles activated by stretch, chemical substances, or drugs → bradycardia, hypotension, apnea, or cardiac arrest
  • Can occur during regional anesthesia, upper-extremity nerve blocks, and general anesthesia; most commonly in the sitting position during UE blocks
  • Ensure sufficient cardiac monitoring; complications can be overcome with necessary precautions

Local Anesthetic Systemic Toxicity (LAST)

  • Avoid fast, forceful injection; ↓ volumes in elderly/frail
  • Most common causes:
    • Inadvertent intravascular injection (vertebral or carotid → rapid-onset seizures)
    • Vertebral artery at C6 — often visible on US
    • Channeling of forceful injection into small veins or lymphatic channels

Nerve Injury

  • Never inject with excessive pressure (>15 psi)
  • Stop if pt reports severe pain or withdrawal reaction

Total Spinal Anesthesia

  • Needle enters intervertebral foramen → LA into dural sleeves
  • Intervertebral foramen = the opening between every two vertebrae where the nerve roots exit the spine; ISB needle is very close to these exits

NEVER inject if stimulation obtained at <0.2 mA — avoids injection into dural sleeves

Hematoma / Vascular Puncture

  • Avoid needle insertion through EJV
  • Caution with US probe pressure
  • Avoid multiple needle insertions (atraumatic technique)
  • Vascular puncture uncommon → apply pressure ×5 min if carotid injured
  • Remember: many vascular structures are located in the neck — heightened vigilance required throughout procedure

Infection

  • Maintain strict sterile technique throughout

The interscalene brachial plexus block is the gold-standard regional technique for shoulder surgery. Mastery requires thorough knowledge of brachial plexus anatomy, precise landmark and US identification, careful nerve stimulation thresholds, and vigilant recognition and management of complications.
